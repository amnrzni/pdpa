1. Scope and Applicability

Who Does the Act Apply To? (Section 2)

Coverage

The Personal Data Protection Act 2010 applies to:

  • Commercial transactions: Any person who processes or has control over or authorizes the processing of personal data in respect of commercial transactions
  • Federal Government: The Federal Government (subject to specific exemptions under Section 3)
Key Terminology Change Modified 2024

Important: The 2024 Amendment changed the terminology throughout the Act:

  • "Data User" ‚Üí "Data Controller"
  • "Data User Forum" ‚Üí "Data Controller Forum"
  • "Register of Data Users" ‚Üí "Register of Data Controllers"

Note: This change aligns with international terminology (e.g., GDPR uses "data controller")

Geographical/Jurisdictional Scope

Territorial Application

The Act applies to:

  • Data controllers established in Malaysia
  • Data controllers not established in Malaysia but using equipment in Malaysia for processing personal data (except for transit purposes)

What Types of Data Processing Are Covered?

Processing Definition (Section 4)

"Processing" means collecting, recording, holding, storing, organizing, adapting, altering, retrieving, consulting, using, disclosing, disseminating, aligning, combining, correcting, erasing or destroying personal data.

üí° Practical Examples:
  • ‚úì Covered: E-commerce websites collecting customer information
  • ‚úì Covered: Banks processing customer financial data
  • ‚úì Covered: HR systems managing employee information
  • ‚úì Covered: Marketing agencies maintaining customer databases
  • ‚úì Covered: Healthcare providers storing patient records (commercial)

Sector-Specific Applications

Sector Applicability Special Notes
Private Sector (Commercial) ‚úì Fully Applicable All commercial transactions involving personal data
Federal Government ‚úì Applicable (with exemptions) Subject to Section 3 exemptions
State Governments ‚úó Not Applicable Expressly excluded under Section 3
Personal/Domestic Use ‚úó Not Applicable Individual or household activities excluded

Key Exclusions (Section 3 - Non-application)

What is NOT Covered

The Act does NOT apply to:

  • (a) Information processed by an individual only for personal, family, or household affairs
  • (b) Information processed for the purpose of a credit reporting business under the Credit Reporting Agencies Act 2010
  • (c) Information processed for the purposes of, and is necessary for:
    • (i) Preventing or detecting crime
    • (ii) Apprehending or prosecuting offenders
    • (iii) National security
    • (iv) Defense
  • (d) State Governments, State authorities, and State public authorities
Ambiguity Note: The definition of "commercial transaction" is not explicitly defined in the Act, which may require case-by-case interpretation or further legal consultation for borderline cases.

2. The 7 Data Protection Principles

The Act establishes seven core principles that govern how personal data must be processed. All data controllers must comply with these principles.

Amendment Note Modified 2024

Section 5 - Penalties Increased:

  • Original: Fine up to RM 300,000 or imprisonment up to 2 years or both
  • After 2024 Amendment: Fine up to RM 1,000,000 or imprisonment up to 3 years or both

New Provision: Data processors must also comply with the Security Principle (Section 9) when processing on behalf of data controllers.

Principle 1: General Principle (Section 6)

Core Requirements

Consent Requirement:

  • For regular personal data: A data controller shall not process personal data unless the data subject has given consent
  • For sensitive personal data: Must process according to Section 40 provisions
Exceptions to Consent (Section 6(2))

Personal data may be processed WITHOUT consent when the processing is necessary for:

  1. (a) Performance of a contract to which the data subject is a party
  2. (b) Taking steps at the request of the data subject with a view to entering into a contract
  3. (c) Compliance with any legal obligation (other than contractual)
  4. (d) Protecting the vital interests of the data subject
  5. (e) Administration of justice
  6. (f) Exercise of any functions conferred by law
Data Processing Standards (Section 6(3))

Personal data shall not be processed unless:

  • (a) Processed for a lawful purpose directly related to an activity of the data controller
  • (b) The processing is necessary for or directly related to that purpose
  • (c) The personal data is adequate but not excessive in relation to that purpose
üí° Practical Example - General Principle:

Scenario: An online retailer collects customer information

  • ‚úì Compliant: Collecting name, address, email for order delivery (necessary and adequate)
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Requiring IC number, race, religion when only needed for clothing delivery (excessive)
  • ‚úì Compliant: Processing payment without consent as it's necessary for contract performance
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Sharing data with third-party marketers without consent

Principle 2: Notice and Choice Principle (Section 7)

Mandatory Disclosure Requirements

A data controller must provide written notice to data subjects informing them of:

  1. (a) That personal data is being processed, with a description of the data
  2. (b) The purposes for which the data is being collected and further processed
  3. (c) The source of the personal data (if available)
  4. (d) The data subject's right to request access and correction, and contact information for inquiries
  5. (e) The class of third parties to whom data may be disclosed
  6. (f) Choices and means for limiting processing
  7. (g) Whether supplying the data is obligatory or voluntary
  8. (h) Consequences if the data subject fails to supply obligatory data
Timing of Notice (Section 7(2))

The notice must be given as soon as practicable:

  • When the data subject is first asked to provide personal data, OR
  • When the data controller first collects the personal data, OR
  • Before using the data for a purpose other than collection purpose, OR
  • Before disclosing the data to a third party
Language Requirements (Section 7(3))

Notice must be provided in both Bahasa Malaysia and English, and individuals must have a clear and readily accessible means to exercise their choice in both languages.

üí° Practical Example - Notice and Choice:

Scenario: A mobile app registration form

  • ‚úì Compliant: Privacy notice clearly states data is used for service delivery and may be shared with payment processors
  • ‚úì Compliant: Provides opt-in checkboxes for marketing communications
  • ‚úì Compliant: Privacy policy available in both Bahasa Malaysia and English
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Pre-checked boxes for marketing consent
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Vague "we may share with partners" without specifying classes of third parties

Principle 3: Disclosure Principle (Section 8)

Disclosure Restrictions

Personal data shall NOT be disclosed without the data subject's consent, EXCEPT:

  • For the purpose it was collected, OR a purpose directly related to it
  • To third parties within the classes specified in the notice (Section 7(1)(e))
  • In circumstances permitted under Section 39 (see Exemptions)
ÔøΩÔøΩ Practical Example - Disclosure:

Scenario: A hospital collects patient data for treatment

  • ‚úì Compliant: Sharing with consulting specialists (directly related to treatment)
  • ‚úì Compliant: Disclosing to insurance company if patient consented in notice
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Selling patient database to pharmaceutical companies without consent
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Sharing patient data with hospital's parent company for marketing without consent

Principle 4: Security Principle (Section 9)

Security Requirements Modified 2024

Data controllers must take practical steps to protect personal data from:

  • Loss
  • Misuse
  • Modification
  • Unauthorized or accidental access or disclosure
  • Alteration
  • Destruction

Factors to consider:

  1. Nature of the personal data and harm from security breach
  2. Place or location where data is stored
  3. Security measures in equipment storing the data
  4. Measures for ensuring reliability, integrity, and competence of personnel
  5. Measures for ensuring secure data transfer

2024 Amendment: Data processors must also comply with Security Principle when processing on behalf of data controllers (Section 5(1a)).

Increased Penalties Modified 2024
  • Before 2024: Fine up to RM 300,000 or 2 years imprisonment
  • After 2024: Fine up to RM 1,000,000 or 3 years imprisonment
üí° Practical Example - Security:

Scenario: An e-commerce platform stores customer data

  • ‚úì Compliant: Encrypting stored passwords and payment information
  • ‚úì Compliant: Using SSL/TLS for data transmission
  • ‚úì Compliant: Implementing role-based access controls for employee access
  • ‚úì Compliant: Regular security audits and penetration testing
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Storing passwords in plain text
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Allowing all employees unrestricted access to customer databases

Principle 5: Retention Principle (Section 10)

Data Retention Limits

Key Requirements:

  • Personal data shall NOT be kept longer than necessary for the fulfillment of its purpose
  • Data controllers have a duty to take all reasonable steps to destroy or permanently delete personal data when no longer required
ÔøΩÔøΩ Practical Example - Retention:

Scenario: HR department maintains employee records

  • ‚úì Compliant: Retaining employment records for 7 years as required by law
  • ‚úì Compliant: Deleting job application data of rejected candidates after 1 year
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Keeping all employee data indefinitely "just in case"
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Retaining detailed customer browsing history for 10 years when only needed for 6 months

Principle 6: Data Integrity Principle (Section 11)

Accuracy Requirements

Data controllers must take reasonable steps to ensure personal data is:

  • Accurate
  • Complete
  • Not misleading
  • Kept up-to-date

This must be considered in relation to the purpose for which the data was collected and further processed.

üí° Practical Example - Data Integrity:

Scenario: A telecommunications company maintains customer accounts

  • ‚úì Compliant: Prompting customers to update contact details annually
  • ‚úì Compliant: Verifying address changes before updating
  • ‚úì Compliant: Implementing data validation during input
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Never updating customer records even when notified of changes
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Using outdated customer information for credit decisions

Principle 7: Access Principle (Section 12)

Data Subject Access Rights Modified 2024

Data subjects have the right to:

  • (a) Be informed whether personal data is being processed
  • (b) Be given a description of the personal data, purposes of processing, and recipients
  • (c) Have communicated to them in an intelligible form:
    • The personal data being processed
    • Information about the source of the data
  • (d) Be given information about the data processing mechanism (to the extent it can be communicated intelligibly)
  • (e) Request correction of inaccurate, incomplete, misleading, or not up-to-date personal data
  • (f) Request withdrawal of consent (added in 2024)
  • (g) Request data portability New 2024
New Right: Data Portability New 2024

The 2024 Amendment introduced the right to data portability, allowing data subjects to receive their personal data in a structured, commonly used format and to transmit this data to another data controller.

üí° Practical Example - Access:

Scenario: Customer requests access to their data from a bank

  • ‚úì Compliant: Providing data within 21 days (as per regulations)
  • ‚úì Compliant: Providing data in intelligible format (e.g., clear report, not raw database dump)
  • ‚úì Compliant (2024): Providing data in portable format (e.g., CSV, JSON) upon request
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Charging excessive fees for data access
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Ignoring or delaying access requests without valid reason
Note on Transitional Period: The 2024 Amendment allows the Minister to appoint different dates for different provisions to come into operation. Organizations should monitor official gazettes for implementation dates.

3. Rights of Data Subjects

Under the PDPA, individuals (data subjects) have specific rights regarding their personal data. These rights are detailed in Division 4 of the Act (Sections 30-40).

3.1 Right to Access Personal Data (Section 30)

Access Request Rights

A data subject may make a "data access request" to a data controller to:

  • Be informed whether the data controller is processing personal data about them
  • Be given a description of the personal data, purposes, and potential recipients
  • Have access to the personal data
  • Be informed about the data processing mechanism

3.2 Compliance with Access Requests (Section 31)

Data Controller Obligations

Upon receiving a data access request, the data controller must:

  • Inform the requestor whether personal data is being processed
  • Comply with the request as soon as practicable (typically within 21 days)
  • Provide the information in intelligible form
  • May charge a prescribed fee (must not exceed cost of compliance)

3.3 When Access Can Be Refused (Section 32)

Grounds for Refusal

A data controller may refuse a data access request if:

  • (a) The request is not made in accordance with Section 30
  • (b) Complying would reveal another person's personal data (except with consent or if reasonable)
  • (c) The data is subject to legal professional privilege
  • (d) The request is frivolous or vexatious
  • (e) The fee has not been paid
  • (f) Other circumstances prescribed by Minister

3.4 Right to Correct Personal Data (Section 34)

Correction Rights Enhanced 2024

A data subject may request correction of personal data that is:

  • Inaccurate
  • Incomplete
  • Misleading
  • Not up-to-date

The data controller must correct the data as soon as practicable and send corrected data to third parties to whom it was disclosed (unless impracticable).

3.5 Right to Withdraw Consent (Section 38)

Consent Withdrawal

Data subjects have the right to withdraw consent at any time by written notice, unless:

  • Withdrawal is prohibited by law
  • It's contrary to a contract between the parties

Effect of withdrawal: The data controller must cease processing unless another lawful basis exists.

3.6 Right to Data Portability (New in 2024)

Data Portability Right New 2024

The 2024 Amendment introduced the right to data portability by:

  • Expanding the definition of "requestor" to include "data portability request"
  • Allowing data subjects to receive their data in a structured, commonly used, and machine-readable format
  • Enabling data subjects to transmit their data to another data controller

Note: Detailed implementation regulations may follow.

3.7 Limitations and Conditions

Right Time Frame Key Limitations
Access Request 21 days (typical) May be refused if frivolous, vexatious, or affects others' privacy
Correction Request As soon as practicable Only for inaccurate/incomplete data; may require evidence
Withdraw Consent Immediate upon notice Not applicable if prohibited by law or contractual obligation
Data Portability TBD (New 2024) Subject to regulations and technical feasibility

3.8 How to Exercise Rights

Procedural Requirements

To exercise rights under the PDPA:

  1. Submit written request: Usually via form provided by data controller or written letter/email
  2. Provide identification: To verify identity and prevent unauthorized access
  3. Specify the right: Clearly state whether requesting access, correction, withdrawal, or portability
  4. Pay fee if applicable: For access requests, reasonable fees may apply
  5. Await response: Data controller must respond within reasonable time (typically 21 days)
üí° Practical Example - Exercising Rights:

Scenario: Customer wants to access data from online retailer

  1. Customer submits data access request via company's online form
  2. Company acknowledges request within 48 hours
  3. Customer provides copy of IC for verification
  4. Company provides comprehensive data report within 14 days
  5. Customer notices incorrect address and submits correction request
  6. Company corrects data within 7 days and confirms correction
Complaint Rights: If a data controller refuses a request or fails to respond, data subjects can lodge a complaint with the Personal Data Protection Commissioner (see Enforcement section).

4. Obligations of Data Controllers

Data controllers (formerly "data users") have various obligations under the PDPA, including registration, appointing data protection officers, and maintaining records.

4.1 Registration Requirements (Division 2, Sections 13-20)

Who Must Register

Classes of data controllers prescribed by the Minister must register with the Personal Data Protection Commissioner.

Note: Not all data controllers are required to register - only those in classes specified by ministerial regulations.

Registration Process (Sections 14-16)

The registration process involves:

  1. Submit application to Commissioner in prescribed form
  2. Include prescribed information and pay prescribed fee
  3. Commissioner issues certificate of registration if satisfied
  4. Certificate valid for prescribed period (subject to renewal)
Register of Data Controllers (Section 20)

The Commissioner maintains a public Register of Data Controllers containing:

  • Name and address of each registered data controller
  • Description of personal data processed
  • Purpose of processing
  • Description of data subjects
  • Classes of third parties to whom data may be disclosed
  • Countries/territories to which data may be transferred

4.2 Data Controller Forums and Codes of Practice (Division 3, Sections 21-29)

Industry Self-Regulation Terminology Updated 2024

Data Controller Forums (formerly "Data User Forums") can be established to promote self-regulation within industries.

Functions:

  • Develop and submit codes of practice to the Commissioner
  • Promote compliance with PDPA within their sector
  • Provide guidance to members

Codes of Practice:

  • Must be approved by the Commissioner
  • Provide sector-specific guidance on PDPA compliance
  • Failure to comply with code may be taken into account in proceedings

4.4 Data Processor Obligations

Data Processor Security Duty New 2024

The 2024 Amendment introduced explicit obligations for data processors:

  • Security Principle compliance: Data processors must comply with Section 9 (Security Principle) when processing on behalf of data controllers
  • Penalty: Violations subject to same increased penalties (up to RM 1,000,000 or 3 years imprisonment)

This clarifies that data processors share responsibility for data security.

Documentation Obligations

While not explicitly detailed in one section, data controllers should maintain:

  • Processing records: Types of data, purposes, recipients
  • Consent records: Evidence of consent obtained from data subjects
  • Security measures: Documentation of technical and organizational security
  • Data breach records: Incidents, responses, and notifications
  • Request logs: Access, correction, and other data subject requests
  • Third-party agreements: Contracts with data processors and other third parties

5. Cross-Border Data Transfer

The PDPA regulates the transfer of personal data outside Malaysia to ensure adequate protection is maintained.

5.1 General Prohibition and Conditions (Section 129)

Transfer Restrictions

General Rule: Personal data shall NOT be transferred to a place outside Malaysia unless that place ensures a level of protection comparable to Malaysia.

How to determine "adequate protection":

  • Examine the data protection laws of the receiving country
  • Consider international agreements or arrangements
  • Assess security measures at the receiving location
  • Review contractual provisions for data protection

5.2 Permitted Transfer Mechanisms

Ways to Transfer Data Abroad

Personal data may be transferred outside Malaysia if:

  1. Adequate protection exists: The destination has comparable data protection standards
  2. Consent obtained: The data subject has consented to the transfer
  3. Contractual necessity: Transfer is necessary for performance of a contract with the data subject
  4. Legal obligation: Transfer is required by law
  5. Public interest: Transfer is in the public interest
  6. Legal proceedings: Transfer is for establishing, exercising, or defending legal rights
  7. Vital interests: Transfer is necessary to protect vital interests of the data subject

5.3 Standard Contractual Clauses

Contractual Safeguards

Where a destination country lacks adequate data protection laws, organizations often use:

  • Data Transfer Agreements: Contracts that impose PDPA-level obligations on the recipient
  • Standard Contractual Clauses: Pre-approved clauses that ensure adequate protection
  • Binding Corporate Rules: Internal policies for multinational organizations

Note: The Commissioner may provide guidance on acceptable contractual terms.

5.4 Whitelisted Countries/Regions

Recognized Jurisdictions

Ministerial Authority: The Minister may prescribe countries or territories deemed to have adequate data protection standards.

Current Status: As of the time of this analysis, no official whitelist has been publicly released. Organizations should:

  • Monitor official gazettes for updates
  • Conduct individual adequacy assessments
  • Seek legal advice for specific transfers
Practical Guidance: Countries with GDPR-level protection (EU member states) or comprehensive data protection laws (e.g., Singapore, Australia, South Korea) are generally considered to provide adequate protection, though formal determination by Malaysian authorities is advisable.

6. Sensitive Personal Data

Sensitive personal data receives heightened protection under the PDPA due to its sensitive nature and potential for discrimination or harm.

6.1 Definition and Categories (Section 4)

What is Sensitive Personal Data? Expanded 2024

"Sensitive personal data" means personal data consisting of information as to:

  1. Physical or mental health or condition
  2. Political opinions
  3. Religious beliefs or other beliefs of a similar nature
  4. Commission or alleged commission of any offence
  5. New 2024 Biometric data

Biometric Data Definition: Any personal data resulting from technical processing relating to the physical, physiological, or behavioural characteristics of a person.

2024 Amendment - Biometric Data Added New 2024

The 2024 Amendment explicitly added biometric data to the definition of sensitive personal data.

Examples of biometric data:

  • Fingerprints
  • Facial recognition data
  • Iris scans
  • Voice prints
  • DNA data
  • Gait analysis
  • Behavioral patterns (typing rhythm, signature dynamics)

Impact: This addition reflects growing concerns about biometric technologies and brings Malaysia in line with international standards like GDPR.

6.2 Additional Protections Required (Section 40)

Heightened Standards for Processing

General Rule: Sensitive personal data shall NOT be processed except in accordance with Section 40.

Section 40 - Processing of Sensitive Personal Data:

A data controller may process sensitive personal data about a data subject ONLY if:

  1. (a) The data subject has given explicit consent to the processing
  2. (b) The processing is necessary:
    • (i) For purposes of exercising/performing legal rights or obligations
    • (ii) To protect vital interests of the data subject
    • (iii) For administration of justice
    • (iv) For medical purposes and is undertaken by health professional or equivalent person
    • (v) For specified purposes in the public interest

6.3 Special Processing Conditions

Data Type Consent Requirement Special Conditions
Health Information Explicit consent Medical purposes by healthcare professional; or vital interests
Political Opinions Explicit consent Public interest purposes (e.g., electoral administration)
Religious Beliefs Explicit consent Limited exceptions for religious organizations
Criminal Offences Explicit consent Administration of justice; crime prevention
Biometric Data (New 2024) Explicit consent Same as other sensitive data; identity verification purposes

6.4 Explicit Consent vs. Regular Consent

Higher Standard for Sensitive Data

Regular Consent: For non-sensitive personal data

  • May be implied from conduct in certain circumstances
  • Opt-out mechanisms may be acceptable in some cases

Explicit Consent: Required for sensitive personal data

  • Must be clear, specific, and unambiguous
  • Should be opt-in (not pre-checked boxes)
  • Must be separately obtained for sensitive data
  • Should be documented and easily provable
üí° Practical Examples - Sensitive Data:

Example 1: Healthcare App

  • ‚úì Compliant: Separate consent checkbox: "I consent to processing of my health data for medical consultation purposes"
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: General terms: "I agree to the processing of all my information"

Example 2: Biometric Attendance System (New context after 2024)

  • ‚úì Compliant: Explicit consent form explaining fingerprint data is sensitive, purpose is attendance tracking, and how it's protected
  • ‚úì Compliant: Offering alternative non-biometric methods for those who refuse
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Mandatory fingerprint scanning without explicit consent or alternatives

Example 3: Financial Institution

  • ‚úì Compliant: Collecting health information for insurance purposes with explicit consent
  • ‚úì Compliant: Processing criminal record data for AML compliance (legal obligation)
  • ‚úó Non-compliant: Requesting religious information for profiling purposes
Key Takeaway for Biometric Data (2024): The addition of biometric data to the sensitive data category means organizations using fingerprint scanners, facial recognition, or other biometric systems must now obtain explicit consent and provide heightened protection. This has significant implications for access control systems, time attendance systems, and identity verification processes.

6.5 Security Requirements for Sensitive Data

Enhanced Security Measures

While Section 9 (Security Principle) applies to all personal data, sensitive personal data requires particularly robust security due to greater potential harm from breach:

  • Stronger encryption: Both at rest and in transit
  • Stricter access controls: Limit to essential personnel only
  • Enhanced monitoring: Regular audits and logging of access
  • Pseudonymization/Anonymization: Where possible and appropriate
  • Regular security assessments: More frequent testing and updates

7. Enforcement & Penalties

The PDPA establishes a comprehensive enforcement framework with criminal penalties, administrative fines, and regulatory powers.

7.1 The Personal Data Protection Commissioner

Regulatory Authority (Part V - Sections 41-44)

Role: The Personal Data Protection Commissioner is appointed to administer and enforce the PDPA.

Key Powers:

  • Investigate complaints and suspected violations
  • Conduct inspections and audits
  • Require production of documents and information
  • Issue enforcement notices
  • Impose administrative fines
  • Refer criminal matters for prosecution
  • Approve codes of practice
  • Issue guidelines and best practice recommendations

7.2 Criminal Penalties (Various sections)

Principle Violations Penalties Increased 2024

7.3 Administrative Penalties

Administrative Fines

In addition to criminal penalties, the Commissioner may impose administrative fines:

  • Maximum: RM 500,000 per violation
  • Purpose: For less serious violations or regulatory non-compliance
  • Process: Administrative determination without criminal proceedings
  • Appeal: Subject to appeal to the Appeal Tribunal

7.4 Enforcement Powers

Commissioner's Enforcement Tools (Part VI)

Investigation Powers:

  1. Inspection: Enter and inspect premises (with warrant if necessary)
  2. Document Production: Require production of documents, records, and data
  3. Information Requests: Compel provision of information and explanations
  4. Search and Seizure: Seize evidence of violations
  5. Interviews: Examine persons under investigation

Enforcement Actions:

  1. Enforcement Notice: Direct data controller to take or cease action
  2. Administrative Fine: Impose monetary penalty up to RM 500,000
  3. Criminal Referral: Refer matter to police for criminal investigation
  4. Public Statement: Issue public statements about violations
  5. Registration Revocation: Cancel registration certificate

7.5 Complaint Process

How to File a Complaint

Data subjects or others may lodge complaints about PDPA violations:

  1. Submit complaint: To the Personal Data Protection Department
  2. Provide details: Nature of violation, parties involved, evidence
  3. Commissioner reviews: Determines if investigation warranted
  4. Investigation: Commissioner may investigate and gather evidence
  5. Resolution: Enforcement action, mediation, or dismissal
  6. Appeal: Decisions may be appealed to the Appeal Tribunal

7.6 Appeal Mechanism

Personal Data Protection Appeal Tribunal

The Act establishes an Appeal Tribunal to hear appeals against:

  • Commissioner's decisions on complaints
  • Administrative fines imposed
  • Enforcement notices issued
  • Registration refusals or cancellations

Further Appeal: Tribunal decisions may be appealed to the High Court on questions of law.

üí° Practical Example - Enforcement:

Scenario: Data Breach at Retail Company

  1. Incident: Retail company suffers data breach affecting 50,000 customers
  2. Investigation: Commissioner initiates investigation
  3. Findings: Company failed to implement adequate security measures (Section 9 violation)
  4. Enforcement:
    • Criminal prosecution: Potential fine up to RM 1,000,000 or 3 years imprisonment
    • Administrative fine: RM 300,000 imposed
    • Enforcement notice: Directed to implement specific security measures
    • Public statement: Commissioner issues public notice of violation
  5. Outcome: Company complies, implements measures, pays fines
Recent Trend: The 2024 Amendment's significant increase in penalties (from RM 300,000 to RM 1,000,000) signals a more aggressive enforcement posture. Organizations should prioritize compliance to avoid these substantial penalties.

8. Exemptions

The PDPA provides various exemptions from its requirements under specific circumstances.

8.1 Complete Exemptions - Non-Application (Section 3)

Activities NOT Covered by PDPA

The Act does NOT apply to:

  1. (a) Personal/Domestic Processing:
    • Processing by an individual only for personal, family, or household affairs
    • Example: Personal address book, family photos
  2. (b) Credit Reporting:
    • Information processed for credit reporting business under Credit Reporting Agencies Act 2010
    • Example: CTOS, CCRIS, credit bureaus
  3. (c) National Security & Law Enforcement:

    Processing necessary for:

    • (i) Preventing or detecting crime
    • (ii) Apprehending or prosecuting offenders
    • (iii) National security
    • (iv) Defense

    Example: Police investigations, military operations, intelligence activities

  4. (d) State Governments:
    • State Governments, State authorities, and State public authorities
    • Note: Federal Government IS subject to PDPA (with limited exemptions)

8.2 Partial Exemptions (Section 45)

Exemptions from Specific Principles

The Minister may prescribe exemptions from specific provisions for:

  • Categories of data controllers or processors
  • Types of personal data
  • Specific processing purposes

Common Partial Exemptions:

Purpose Exempted Principles Conditions
Statistical/Research General, Notice, Disclosure, Access Data not processed for other purposes; results don't identify individuals
News/Journalism Various (as prescribed) Processing solely for journalistic purposes; public interest
Legal Proceedings Disclosure Principle Disclosure necessary for court proceedings or legal advice
Emergency Situations General, Notice, Disclosure Necessary to protect vital interests; prevent serious harm

8.3 Specific Exemption Scenarios

Journalism & Media

Purpose: Balance data protection with freedom of expression and public interest in journalism.

  • Exemption: Processing solely for journalistic, literary, or artistic purposes
  • Condition: Processing must be in the public interest
  • Limitation: Exemption does not extend to commercial use of personal data
Statistical Research

Purpose: Enable legitimate research while protecting individual privacy.

  • Exemption: From General, Notice, Disclosure, and Access Principles
  • Conditions:
    • Data processed only for statistical or research purposes
    • Data not processed for any other purpose
    • Results published in form that doesn't identify individuals
Legal Proceedings

Purpose: Facilitate administration of justice and legal rights.

  • Exemption: From disclosure restrictions
  • Applies to:
    • Court proceedings and tribunal hearings
    • Legal advice from lawyers
    • Exercise/defense of legal rights

8.4 Exemptions vs. Exceptions

Understanding the Distinction

Exemptions (Sections 3, 45):

  • PDPA does not apply at all, OR
  • Specific principles don't apply
  • Typically for categories of processing or processors

Exceptions (within principles):

  • PDPA applies but specific requirement is waived in certain circumstances
  • Example: Consent not required when processing necessary for contract performance (Section 6(2))
  • Example: Disclosure permitted without consent in certain circumstances (Section 39)
Category Type Scope Example
State Governments Complete Exemption PDPA doesn't apply State land office processing land titles
Police Investigation Complete Exemption PDPA doesn't apply Criminal investigation data
Research Partial Exemption Some principles don't apply University conducting anonymized research
Contract Processing Exception PDPA applies; consent not needed Processing payment for order
Legal Disclosure Exception PDPA applies; disclosure permitted Responding to court subpoena
üí° Practical Examples - Exemptions:

Example 1: News Media

  • Scenario: Newspaper publishes article about public figure
  • Analysis: May be exempt from PDPA if processing solely for journalistic purposes and in public interest
  • Limitation: Exemption doesn't cover selling subscriber data to advertisers (commercial purpose)

Example 2: Academic Research

  • Scenario: University conducts health research using patient data
  • Analysis: May be exempt from certain principles if data anonymized and used only for research
  • Requirement: Published results must not identify individual data subjects

Example 3: Federal vs. State Government

  • Federal Agency: Subject to PDPA (e.g., federal hospital must comply)
  • State Agency: NOT subject to PDPA (e.g., state hospital exempt)
  • Impact: Creates different privacy standards for federal vs. state entities
Ambiguity Note: The exemption for State Governments but not Federal Government creates complexity. Private sector organizations contracted by state governments should seek legal clarification on whether the exemption extends to them.

8.5 Ministerial Power to Prescribe Exemptions

Future Exemptions

Section 45 grants the Minister broad power to prescribe additional exemptions:

  • May exempt specific categories of data controllers or processors
  • May exempt specific types of personal data or processing
  • Exemptions may be conditional or unconditional
  • Organizations should monitor official gazettes for new exemptions

üìñ Glossary - Legal Terms in Plain Language

This glossary translates legal and technical terms from the PDPA into everyday language to help non-legal professionals understand the Act.

Personal Data
Legal definition: Information that relates directly or indirectly to a data subject who is identified or identifiable from that information or from that and other information in the possession of a data user.

Plain language: Any information about a person that can be used to identify them, either on its own or combined with other information. Examples: name, IC number, phone number, email address, IP address, photo, fingerprint.
Data Controller (formerly Data User)
Legal definition: A person who either alone or jointly with other persons processes personal data or has control over or authorizes the processing of any personal data.

Plain language: The organization or person who decides how and why personal data is collected and used. They're in charge of the data. Example: A company that collects customer information for its business.
Data Processor
Legal definition: Any person, other than an employee of the data user, who processes the personal data solely on behalf of the data user.

Plain language: A third-party company or service that processes data on behalf of the data controller, but doesn't make decisions about how to use it. Example: A cloud storage provider, payroll service bureau, or IT vendor.
Data Subject
Legal definition: The individual who is the subject of the personal data.

Plain language: The person whose information is being collected or used. You, me, customers, employees - anyone whose data is being processed. Note: Does not include deceased individuals (as of 2024 Amendment).
Processing
Legal definition: Collecting, recording, holding, storing, organizing, adapting, altering, retrieving, consulting, using, disclosing, disseminating, aligning, combining, correcting, erasing or destroying data.

Plain language: Basically anything you do with personal data - from collecting it, to storing it, using it, sharing it, or deleting it. Almost any handling of personal data is "processing".
Sensitive Personal Data
Legal definition: Personal data consisting of information as to physical or mental health, political opinions, religious beliefs, commission of offences, or biometric data (added 2024).

Plain language: Extra-private information that could cause serious harm if misused, like health records, religious beliefs, political views, criminal history, or biometric data (fingerprints, facial recognition). This data gets special protection.
Biometric Data (New 2024)
Legal definition: Any personal data resulting from technical processing relating to the physical, physiological or behavioural characteristics of a person.

Plain language: Data from physical characteristics used to identify someone, like fingerprints, facial features, iris patterns, voice prints, or DNA. This is now classified as sensitive personal data.
Consent
Legal definition: Any freely given specific and informed indication of wishes by which the data subject signifies his agreement to the processing of personal data relating to him.

Plain language: Permission that is freely given, specific to a purpose, and based on clear information. Can't be forced or hidden in fine print. Must be genuine agreement.
Explicit Consent
Legal definition: Not explicitly defined but required for sensitive personal data.

Plain language: Super-clear permission, usually requiring active opt-in (like checking a box) rather than passive agreement. Must be specific, unambiguous, and separately obtained for sensitive data. No pre-checked boxes allowed.
Personal Data Breach (New 2024)
Legal definition: Any breach of personal data, loss of personal data, misuse of personal data or unauthorized access of personal data.

Plain language: When something goes wrong with personal data - it's hacked, leaked, stolen, lost, or accessed by someone who shouldn't have it. Example: A laptop with customer data is stolen, or a database is hacked.
Data Portability (New 2024)
Legal definition: The right to receive personal data in a structured, commonly used format and to transmit to another data controller.

Plain language: Your right to get your data from one company and move it to another company, like downloading your data from one bank and uploading to another. Think of it like changing phone numbers and taking your contacts with you.
Commercial Transaction
Legal definition: Not explicitly defined in the Act.

Plain language: Business activities - buying, selling, trading, providing services for money or value. The Act mainly applies to businesses, not personal activities. (Note: Ambiguous area - seek legal advice for borderline cases)
Third Party
Legal definition: Any person other than the data subject, the data user or data processor, or any person authorized by the data user or data processor to process data.

Plain language: Someone outside your organization who you might share data with - like partners, vendors, service providers, or government agencies. Not your employees or contractors who work for you.
Class of Third Parties
Legal definition: Categories or groups of third parties to whom data may be disclosed.

Plain language: Types of organizations you might share data with, not necessarily naming specific companies. Example: "payment processors," "delivery companies," "marketing agencies" rather than "ABC Payment Sdn Bhd."
Adequate Protection
Legal definition: A level of protection comparable to that provided under the PDPA.

Plain language: When transferring data to another country, that country must have data protection laws and practices as good as Malaysia's. Think of it as "our rules or equally good rules."
Enforcement Notice
Legal definition: A notice issued by the Commissioner directing a data user to take or cease certain actions.

Plain language: An official order from the regulator telling you to do something or stop doing something. Like a "cease and desist" or "fix this problem" order. Ignoring it can lead to penalties.
Data Access Request
Legal definition: A request by a data subject to a data controller for access to personal data.

Plain language: When you ask a company "What information do you have about me?" They must respond and show you what they've got (with some exceptions).
Data Correction Request
Legal definition: A request by a data subject to correct personal data that is inaccurate, incomplete, misleading or not up-to-date.

Plain language: Asking a company to fix wrong information they have about you. Like updating your address, correcting a misspelled name, or fixing an error in your account.
Code of Practice
Legal definition: A code prepared by a Data User Forum and approved by the Commissioner for a particular sector.

Plain language: Industry-specific rules developed by businesses in that sector (like banking or healthcare) and approved by the regulator. Like a "how to comply with PDPA in our industry" guide.
Pseudonymization
Legal definition: Not explicitly defined in PDPA.

Plain language: Replacing identifying information with fake names or codes, so you can't easily tell who the data is about without additional information kept separately. Like using "Customer #12345" instead of "Ahmad bin Ali."
Anonymization
Legal definition: Not explicitly defined in PDPA.

Plain language: Removing all identifying information so it's impossible (or extremely difficult) to figure out who the data is about. Once truly anonymized, data is no longer "personal data" under PDPA.
